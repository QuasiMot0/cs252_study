## Why Fenceposts Are Needed

When the allocator frees a block at the **edge of a heap chunk**, the coalescing code will compute the address of the left or right neighbor. Without protection, this neighbor address points to memory **outside the chunk** — either before the start of the chunk or past its end. Reading/writing that memory causes undefined behavior.

From the slides:
> "If the object freed is at the beginning of the heap, it is likely that your allocator will try erroneously to coalesce memory beyond the beginning of the heap."

A second issue: other libraries may call `sbrk()`, creating **holes in the heap** — non-contiguous gaps between the allocator's chunks. The allocator must not try to coalesce across such gaps.

## The Fencepost Solution

Every time a new chunk is obtained from the OS, the allocator places **dummy headers** at the chunk's edges:

- **At the beginning of the chunk**: a dummy footer (fencepost) with `allocated = 1`.
- **At the end of the chunk**: a dummy header (fencepost) with `allocated = 1`.

Since fenceposts appear allocated, the coalescing code naturally stops when it reaches one — it sees `allocated = 1` and does not merge.

From the slides:
> "To prevent coalescing with memory beyond the beginning or end of the chunks that belong to the heap, every time a chunk of memory is requested from the OS your allocator has to add a dummy footer at the beginning of the chunk with the flag set to 'allocated'. Also at the end of the chunk you will add a header with the flag set to 'allocated'."

## Fencepost Layout

```
Chunk from OS:
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ [Fencepost A] │ Free Block │ [Fencepost B] │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────┘
  (allocated=1)   (free blocks)   (allocated=1)
```

The first free block in the chunk has `left_size` equal to `sizeof(fencepost_A)`.

## When to Remove Fenceposts

If two consecutive `sbrk()` calls return **adjacent** (contiguous) chunks, the fenceposts between them are unnecessary and wasteful. The allocator should remove them and coalesce the end of the first chunk with the beginning of the new chunk.

From the slides:
> "If two chunks are consecutive, you should remove the fence posts between them."

From the lab spec, when the new chunk is adjacent to the previous chunk:
1. Remove the fencepost at the **end** of the previous chunk (Fencepost B).
2. Remove the fencepost at the **beginning** of the new chunk (Fencepost A).
3. Coalesce the last free block of the previous chunk with the free space of the new chunk.

## Lab Diagram

```
Before (two separate chunks):
  [FP-A][Block...][FP-B]  [FP-A][Block...][FP-B]

After coalescing adjacent chunks:
  [FP-A][Block.........(merged)..........][FP-B]
```

The merged region can then be split to satisfy future allocation requests.

## Key Terms

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Fencepost | A dummy block header/footer with `allocated=1` at chunk edges; stops coalescing at chunk boundaries |
| Chunk start fencepost | Prevents coalescing backward beyond the start of a chunk |
| Chunk end fencepost | Prevents coalescing forward beyond the end of a chunk |
| Adjacent chunks | Two sbrk() calls returning contiguous memory; their shared fenceposts must be removed |
| Heap hole | A gap in the heap caused by an external `sbrk()` call; fenceposts prevent crossing it |
