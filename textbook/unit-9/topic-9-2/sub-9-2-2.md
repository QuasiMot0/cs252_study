## The Core Trade-off

From the slides:
> "The shorter the quantum, the shorter the average completion time."
> "We are not considering that context switch takes time."
> "We have to consider the context switch overhead."

Making the quantum very small improves response time but increases the fraction of CPU time wasted on context switches.

## Quantum Length Analysis: Example

From the slides (T=10ms case):

| Process | Burst Time |
|---|---|
| p1 | 20ms |
| p2 | 1ms |
| p3 | 1ms |

With **T=10ms**: P1(10) → P2(1) → P3(1) → P1(10)
**Average completion time = (11 + 12 + 22) / 3 = 15ms**

With **T=1ms**: P1(1) → P2(1) → P3(1) → P1(1) → P1(1) → P1(1) → P1(1) → …
**Average completion time = (2 + 3 + 22) / 3 = 9ms**

The smaller quantum gives better average completion time — but ignores context switch cost.

## Context Switch Overhead Formula

From the slides:
> "The context switch overhead is the time it takes to do a context switch as a portion of the quantum time."

```
Xoverhd% = 100 * X / T

where:
  Xoverhd% = Context Switch Overhead (%)
  X = Context Switch Time
  T = Quantum Time
```

Assuming X = 0.1ms:

| Quantum T | Overhead |
|---|---|
| T = 10ms | 100 × 0.1 / 10 = **1%** |
| T = 2ms | 100 × 0.1 / 2 = **5%** |
| T = 0.2ms | 100 × 0.1 / 0.2 = **50%** |

At T=0.2ms, half the CPU time is spent context switching rather than doing useful work.

## Conclusions

From the slides:
> "The smaller the quantum, the faster the response time (small average completion time) but the larger the context switch overhead."
> "The larger the quantum the smaller the context switch overhead but the larger the response (large average completion time)."
> "The standard quantum time is 1/100sec = 10ms that is a compromise between response time and context switch overhead. This may change in the future with faster processors."

## CPU Burst Distribution

From the slides:
> "Processes are in waiting state most of the time except for the times they need the CPU, that is usually for short periods of time."
> "These short periods of time the processes need the CPU are called CPU bursts."
> "90% of CPU bursts are smaller than 10ms."
> "By choosing 10ms to be the quantum length, we make sure that 90% of the CPU burst run until completion without being preempted."
> "This reduces the context switch overhead and reduces the completion time and makes the response time faster."

The 10ms quantum is chosen specifically because it is large enough for 90% of CPU bursts to complete without interruption, minimizing unnecessary context switches.

## How to Choose the Quantum Size

From the slides:
> "Make the quantum small enough to make the response time smaller (faster)."
> "Make the quantum large enough to have an acceptable context switch overhead."
> "Make the quantum large enough so most CPU bursts are able to finish without being preempted."

## Key Terms

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Time quantum (T) | Duration of each scheduling slice in round-robin |
| Context switch time (X) | Time required to save and restore process state |
| Context switch overhead | Xoverhd% = 100 × X / T; fraction of CPU wasted on switching |
| CPU burst | Short period when a process needs the CPU between I/O waits |
| 10ms standard quantum | 1/100 sec; compromise chosen because 90% of CPU bursts are under 10ms |
| Response time | Average time for a process to complete after arriving in the ready queue |
